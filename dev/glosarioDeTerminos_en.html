Glossary of Terms

Load Lifting

Load lifting consists of a set of logistical maneuvers aimed at moving large objects using cranes or other types of machinery. Generally, load lifting is used when it is not possible to move or transport merchandise manually due to various factors, such as its dimensions, fragility, or the responsibility it represents for the end customer.

Rigger

A person authorized, trained, and responsible for giving maneuvering signals to the crane operator, inspecting and selecting the slings and rigging accessories, slinging the load, and ensuring safety conditions are appropriate.

Working Load Limit (WLL)

This is the maximum load the product can withstand without altering its physical properties during its lifespan.

Safe Working Load (SWL)

This is the maximum load the product can safely withstand depending on the rigging configuration.

Proof Load

This is the average force to which the product is subjected before any deformation is observed.

Breaking Load

This is the load applied to the product, at which point it fails or does not hold the load.

Dynamic Load

A force applied suddenly (impacts or jerks), causing a significant increase in static load.

Safety Factor

Theoretical reserve of the product, resulting from dividing the breaking load by the working load limit.

Center of Gravity

The center of gravity of a body or system is the point where all the gravitational force acting on the mass of that body or system is considered to be concentrated. In other words, the center of gravity of a body is the point where the gravitational force exerted by the Earth on that body is applied.

Risks Associated with a Load Lifting Maneuver

The risks and hazard factors most commonly present in tasks performed with these work equipment include:
The overturning of the equipment (crane, forklift, manlift, telehandler, etc).
The falling of the load.
Strikes against objects or people.
Entrapment or crushing.
Indirect or direct electrical contacts.

Slings

It is an intermediate device between the load lifting mechanism and the suspended load, intended to secure and handle the load, properly distributing the forces.

Steel Wire Rope Sling

A steel wire rope is a set of wires twisted and grouped helically, forming a metallic rope, suitable for resisting tensile and bending forces. Its simplest and most fundamental components are the WIRES and the CORE. With these elements, more complex structural units called strands are built, which when cabled around their CORE, form the final structure of the steel wire rope.

Steel Wire Rope Sling

A steel wire rope is a set of wires twisted and grouped helically, forming a metallic rope, suitable for resisting tensile and bending forces. Its simplest and most fundamental components are the WIRES and the CORE. With these elements, more complex structural units called strands are built, which when cabled around their CORE, form the final structure of the steel wire rope.

Chain Sling

Steel chain slings in accordance with this standard must be constructed and tested according to ASTM A-391/A-391 M for Grade 80 and according to ASTM A-973/A-973 M for Grade 100.
The minimum design factor is 4.

Shackle

It is a semicircular arc, whose ends are secured with a pin and used to attach a sling.
The design factor for shackles with a capacity of up to 150 tons will be 5.
The design factor for shackles with a capacity greater than 150 tons will be 4.
The shackle will have the manufacturer's brand, capacity, and size stamped on its body.

Force

It is an action that tends to alter the state of rest or constant speed of the body to which it is applied. All forces have magnitude, direction, and sense, in addition to a point of application.

Weight

It corresponds to the force with which the Earth attracts the body, obtained by multiplying the volume by the unit weight or specific weight of the material.

Friction Force

Friction, also known as rubbing force or frictional force, is a force that exists between two surfaces in contact, and opposes motion, meaning it acts in the opposite direction to movement.

Dynamic Forces

Dynamic Force refers to the forces that affect the movement of an object in relation to its mass and acceleration. These forces can be of various types, such as gravity, friction, tension, or air resistance.

Moment or Torque

The moment of a force, also known as torque, dynamic moment, or simply moment, is a vector quantity that measures the ability of a force to alter the rotational speed of a body.

Lateral Wind

Lateral wind is a crucial factor to consider in the operation of telescopic cranes. This type of wind refers to gusts that impact perpendicularly to the crane's boom, which can generate significant forces that affect the stability and safety of the operation.

Tandem

It is a common maneuver within the industrial sector that combines the use of two or more cranes simultaneously to lift the same load. The tandem maneuver is a possibility when the load is too large, requiring the support of another crane.

All-Terrain Crane

All-terrain cranes can be considered a hybrid between truck-mounted cranes and rough terrain (RT) cranes, as they combine the transport capabilities of truck-mounted cranes with the maneuverability of rough terrain cranes.

Truck-Mounted Crane

A truck-mounted crane, also known as a mobile construction crane or loader crane, is a type of crane mounted on a truck or vehicle chassis. It gives the crane the ability to move and lift large loads, transferring them to various locations.

RT Crane

These are versatile and robust machines that provide mobility, stability, and lifting capacity on various terrains and conditions.

Hook

It is an essential component used to lift and move heavy loads. This hook is connected to the crane's telescopic boom, which can extend and retract thanks to a hydraulic mechanism. The boom is made up of several tubes that slide into each other, allowing the length to be adjusted as needed.

Tagline

A securing, towing, or fiber rope usually attached to the load and handled by a person to control the sway or rotation of the load during lifting.

Operating Radius

It refers to the horizontal distance from the center of the crane's rotating base to the point where the load will be lifted.

Angle

It is the angle between the horizontal centerline and the centerline of the boom section (boom's central axis). In all cases, the operating radius will be governed by the crane's capacity.

Boom Elevation Point

It is the vertical distance from the ground to the point of the boom tip's central axis.

Lifting Triangle

It is the triangle formed when observing the load and rigging from the side, comprising an included angle and two horizontal angles that sum up to 180°.

Winch

A set of mechanical elements used to move and lift loads, consisting mainly of a motor connected to a drum that holds a cable.

Pulley

A pulley is a simple machine, a mechanical traction device used to transmit force. It consists of a wheel with a groove in its perimeter through which a rope passes, rotating on a central axis. In addition, forming assemblies—block and tackle—serves to reduce the amount of force needed to move a weight.

Crane Operator

The equipment operator is generally responsible for the operation when the load is on the crane and separated from the ground. Due to the great responsibility, the operator must have enough authority to refuse to perform a maneuver if they deem it unsafe. The lift should only proceed after these concerns have been communicated to the responsible supervisor, discussed, risks identified, and operating conditions ensured.

Rigging

A system of pulleys and cables designed to vary forces and speeds.

Slewing Ring

Elements designed to transmit the forces (load moment, horizontal and vertical forces) from the rotating structure to the crane base, which is driven by the slewing mechanism of the rotating structure.

Rotation Device

A system that ensures the free orientation of the rotating structure against the action of the wind when out of service. It can also be used for the vertical self-alignment of the boom with the load before beginning the lifting maneuver.

Stabilizers

Devices designed to increase and/or ensure the support base of a crane in working position.

Rotating Structure

An orientable structure capable of supporting the boom, counterweight, and all the drive mechanisms of the crane. It does not necessarily house the crane's moving mechanisms.

End-of-Travel Device

A safety device that prevents the block from getting too close to the head of the boom or the JIB.

Boom Angle Indicator

A device that allows reading, from the operator's cabin, the boom's inclination angle at any moment.

Hook Load Indicator

A device that allows reading, from the operator's cabin, the load supported by the pulley or block at any moment.

Boom Length Indicator

A device that allows reading, from the operator's cabin, the boom length at any moment for cranes equipped with a telescopic boom.

Load Moment Indicator

An automatic safety device that detects the maximum load that can be handled for each working position.

Load Limiter

An automatic safety device that detects the maximum load that can be handled for each working position, cutting off the upward movement of the lifting mechanism and those movements that would increase the maximum load moments set in the corresponding load diagram.

Angle Factor

The angle factor is the ratio between the length of the sling measured from the hook seat to the top of the load and the vertical height measured from the hook seat to the top of the load.

Spreader Beams and Equalizers

Spreader beams are used to support long loads during lifting. They eliminate the risk of tilting, sliding, or bending, as well as the possibility of sling angles being quite small (less than 45°), and the tendency of slings to crush the load.

Net Capacity

This is what the crane can lift without taking into account the weight of the hook, quick ball, lifting devices, or any other device connected to the crane that is not considered part of the load.

Gross Capacity

This is what the crane can lift considering the weight of the hook, quick ball, lifting devices, or any other device connected to the crane that is not considered part of the load.

Lateral Load

Load that imposes horizontal forces on the side of the crane's boom.

Swinging Loads

These are loads that, due to improper handling, acquire an oscillatory (pendulum) motion when being lifted.

Center of Rotation

The mark of the center or central point of rotation in the crane from which the lifting radius is measured. It is the center of the circle described by a complete rotation of the crane.

Cocas

They are low-resistance points of steel cable due to the imbalance produced by deformation, and they also suffer premature deterioration due to abrasion.

Operation Quadrant

Operation quadrants are areas concerning the position of the crane, where loads are lifted and deposited, that is, forward, backward, right side, left side. It must be taken into account that depending on the configuration of the crane, they do not have the same capacities in all quadrants.

Deflection

It is the small deformation in an arc shape that the crane's boom undergoes when a load is lifted. The boom regains its original shape when the load is released.

Lifting Dimension

The minimum distance between the suspension level and the support of the lower hook, that is, the distance that must be lifted.

Lifting Equipment

It is equipment composed of a lifting mechanism itself and load transmitters.

Cable Step or Lay

It is the measure to inspect the number of broken wires in a cable. A cable has many lays, and this number depends on the length of the cable. There are several types of lays, and each type is determined by the arrangement of the strands in the cable and how the wires are positioned in the strands. Depending on the arrangement of the strand, it is defined whether the lay is right or left, and depending on the placement of the wires in the strand, it is defined as regular or Lang lay.

Bird Cage

Cable deformations generated by improper operation with loads. This deformation occurs when there is a sudden relaxation of the loads, causing the strands to open and form a kind of cages, displacing the core.

Line of Fire

The term "Line of Fire" simply represents exposure to a hazardous condition, which, if not clearly visualized, involves a high probability of severe damage. Being in the line of fire is the act of interposing our body, or part of it, between, in front of, or below a source of potential energy, significantly greater than what our body can withstand, which can obviously cause us traumatic injury if it reaches us.

Work Zone

Loading, unloading, and storage area for materials from various disciplines. This area must be marked with delimiting tape, cones, or flags, and its extent will be calculated based on twice the length of the largest piece, and in the case of bulky equipment, the radius of the largest piece to be moved will be considered twice.

Operations Manual

An operations manual is the guide on how things are done in a job. It provides an effective way to communicate policies and procedures and gives employees the independence and security they need to operate in their positions, achieving maximum results.

Load Table

It is a table that each crane comes with, in which the manufacturer specifies the capacity of the crane based on the length of the boom, angle of inclination, or swing radius with and without JIB, indicating the safe capacity. This table is fundamental for determining whether the crane to be used is suitable for the load maneuver. The table is specific to each crane and should not be modified.

Rings or Shackles

Also called eye bolts, it is a useful accessory used in crane maneuvers, mainly when lifting an object by pulling directly on it. The shackle is made of forged steel and consists of two parts, a body or eye, which can be male (Din580) or female (Din582), of different sizes for different working loads.

Load Block and/or Hook

Metal structure for mounting pulleys and steel cables, having a hook at the lower end to secure the load. Also known as hook block.

Regular Load

As the name indicates, it is an object whose shape and mass have its center of gravity located at the center of the load.

Irregular Load

An object whose shape and mass do not have its center of gravity at the center of the load, hence it is necessary for the rigger to know the center of gravity of the object to make the correct and safe rigging.

Safety or Design Factor

An industrial term indicating the theoretical reserve capacity of a product, generally calculated by dividing the breaking load by the working load limit, expressed internationally as a ratio, e.g., 5:1, 4:1, etc.

Dynamic Load

Loads that the equipment or its components bear, generated by moving forces.

Nominal Load

Load for which a crane, hoist, winch, or mechanical parts are designed and built.

Live Load

Variable and additional load with an uncertain center of gravity, which can be dangerous for the crane structure.

Calculation Memory

Calculation memory of the maneuver that must consider the lifting equipment capacities, maneuver turning radius, total maneuver weight, dimensions, and maneuver plan.
Calculation memory of auxiliary elements for the maneuver such as ears, yokes, lifting frames.
Calculation memory of the floor where the lifting equipment will be installed.

Synthetic Sling Rejection

Identification of illegible or non-existent sling.
Chemical or acid burns.
Melted or carbonized parts of the sling.
Holes, tears, cuts, or catches.

Chain Sling Rejection

Wear, notches, cracks, breaks, dents, stretching, bends, welding splashes, discoloration due to excess temperature, and hook openings.

Hook Rejection

Plastic deformation: If the dangerous section or neck of the hook has deformed.
Excessive wear: If the hook bushing wear has reached 15% of its original size. If the hook shank is worn to 5% of its original size, it should also be discarded.
Cracks or fissures: Presence of cracks or fissures in the hook.
Twisting: If the hook shows twisting or abnormal wear.
Corrosion: Signs of excessive corrosion compromising the integrity of the hook.

Shackle Rejection

Missing or illegible identification of origin. Evidence of thermal damage. Excessive corrosion. Deformation, cracks, breaks, notches. Loss of section by 10% compared to the original in any part of the body or pin.

Hazard

An act, source, or condition capable of causing damage to people, property, processes, and the environment.

Risk

Probability (Possibility) of a loss occurring when interacting with the hazard.

Ergonomics

Ergonomics is the discipline that focuses on designing workplaces, aiming to optimize the three elements of the system (human-machine-environment) by developing methods for the person, technique, and organization.

Substandard Action

Any act or omission by the worker that diverts them from the accepted way considered correct and safe to carry out an activity or job.

Substandard Condition

A situation or condition of risk created in the workplace.

Law 16.744

PREVENT work accidents and occupational diseases.
PROVIDE medical benefits to cure or heal the worker and restore their work capacity.
PROVIDE economic benefits during the period of incapacity as compensation for the consequences of the accident or occupational disease, including in the case of death.
REHABILITATE the worker to return their earning capabilities fully or partially.
RE-EDUCATE the affected person to give them the possibility to perform a new trade or profession, considering their residual work capacity.

16.744 Company Obligations

Implement Risk Prevention measures indicated by the administering body and/or the Risk Prevention Department and Health & Safety committee.
Establish and keep up to date the Internal Hygiene and Safety Regulations.
Establish the Risk Prevention Department when employing more than 100 workers.
Form a Health & Safety Committee.
Provide Personal Protective Equipment to workers.
Inform workers about occupational risks.

16.744 Workers' Obligations

Comply with what is established in the internal regulations.
Comply with the norms and instructions given by the Health Service, Administering Body, Risk Prevention Department, or Health and Safety Committee.
Use personal protective equipment and/or safety devices.
Attend medical check-ups.
Participate actively in risk prevention activities through the Health and Safety Committee.

ODI - DAS Obligation to Inform / Right to Know D.S. N°40 ART21

Risks involved in their work.
Preventive measures.
Correct working methods.
Elements, products, and substances to be used in production processes or in their work.
Identification of the same (formula, synonyms, appearance, and smell).

Incident

An event that had the potential to produce a loss but alters or interrupts a process.

Work Accident

Any injury suffered by a person as a result of or in connection with work that produces incapacity or death. Accidents that occur directly due to the execution of tasks or labor are considered work-related.
Accidents occurring in connection with work are those in which there is a relationship, even if indirect, between work or labor.
The injury can be physical or psychological.
Injuries caused by overexertion or repetitive motions, which manifest after some time due to their physical work nature, are also considered accidents.

Occupational Diseases

Diseases caused directly by the exercise of the profession or work performed by a person, resulting in incapacity or death.

Near Miss

An incident that did not result in injuries or material damage but would have if it had not been addressed in time.

Lost Time Accident (LTA)

An accident occurring as a result of or in connection with work, where the worker had to take medical rest for one or more days due to temporary incapacity (medical leave type 5 or order for rest).

No Lost Time Accident (NLTA)

An accident occurring as a result of or in connection with work, where the worker suffered minor injuries that allowed them to return to their usual work and shift without requiring rest.

Accidente sin Tiempo Perdido (STP)
